\begin{abstract}
This report presents the exploratory data analysis (EDA) of the \texttt{perpetuals\_knowledge\_graph} database, comprising 40.5 million perpetual futures log events across 447\,599 wallets over 491 days (2025-02-19 to 2026-06-26). We characterise the wallet population, assess the feasibility of Bayesian hierarchical modelling given the trade-count distribution, test the core orthogonality hypothesis (Long vs.\ Short skill), and evaluate leverage, sizing, and cross-asset effects. The results confirm the central research premise---Long and Short skill show only moderate correlation ($r=0.336$), motivating side-aware decomposition---while revealing that the existing baseline composite score is severely compressed ($0.969$--$0.988$ out of $1.0$) and partially redundant. We identify 4\,500 wallets with $\geq20$ closed trades as the Bayesian target population. We then implement the empirical-Bayes hierarchical model (five skill dimensions) and evaluate it against eight baselines. A key methodological finding is that the standard $\geq5$-future-trade evaluation label is $\approx99\%$ binomial sampling noise (reliability $\rho=0.013$), which fully explains the apparent ``luck dominates'' null result; on reliably-labelled wallets the signal is strong. Under rolling multi-fold walk-forward, every Bayesian posterior score beats raw-ROI ranking by $2$--$3\times$ ($r_s\approx0.34$--$0.48$ vs.\ $0.17$), with timing (hit-rate) skill strongest and a learned LambdaMART ranker giving the most regime-stable ranking. The single-fold ``ROI wins'' finding does not survive proper walk-forward.
\end{abstract}

\section{Problem Formulation}
%% ===================================================================

\subsection{Why a Single Composite Score Fails}

Every incumbent wallet-scoring system --- and the baseline in this database (Script 03, \texttt{daily\_trader\_rankings}) --- reduces a wallet $w$'s entire trading history to one scalar:
$$
S_w \;=\; 0.25\cdot\text{RR}_w \;+\; 0.25\cdot\text{WLH}_w \;+\; 0.25\cdot\text{WLR}_w \;+\; 0.25\cdot\text{WP}_w,
$$
an equally-weighted sum of risk/reward ratio, win/loss holding-time ratio, win/loss ROI ratio, and win percentage. Section~\ref{sec:baseline_critique} shows this is empirically pathological: two of the four components are correlated at $r=0.652$ (redundant variance counted twice) and the resulting score is compressed into a $0.969$--$0.988$ band, making rank order nearly meaningless. The deeper issue is conceptual, not just numerical: a scalar $S_w$ cannot distinguish \emph{why} a wallet performs well --- good entries, good exits, correct directional calls, disciplined position sizing, or simply an un-copied idea --- which is exactly the information a copy-trader needs.

\subsection{Formal Decomposition}

Let wallet $w$'s closed-position history be the ordered sequence $\{(a_i, d_i, \ell_i, x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^{N_w}$, where $a_i$ is the traded asset, $d_i \in \{+1,-1\}$ the side (Long/Short), $\ell_i$ the leverage, and $x_i, y_i$ the entry/exit price of trade $i$. The realized return of trade $i$ is
$$
r_i \;=\; d_i\,\frac{y_i - x_i}{x_i}.
$$

\textbf{Timing (hit) skill} is the fraction of directionally correct trades:
$$
\text{HR}_w \;=\; \frac{1}{N_w}\sum_{i=1}^{N_w} \mathbf{1}[r_i > 0].
$$

\textbf{Sizing skill} is the win/loss ratio --- whether size is larger on correct calls:
$$
\text{WL}_w \;=\; \frac{\mathbb{E}[\,r_i \mid r_i>0\,]}{\left|\mathbb{E}[\,r_i \mid r_i \le 0\,]\right|}.
$$

These two are not independent contributions to risk-adjusted return; they multiply. \textbf{Van Loon (2018)} \emph{``Timing versus Sizing Skill,'' Journal of Portfolio Management}, doi:\texttt{10.3905/jpm.2018.44.3.025} --- the original paper this decomposition is taken from --- shows that the Information Ratio factors exactly as
$$
\text{IR}_w \;=\; \text{HR}_w \;\times\; \text{WL}_w \;\times\; \sqrt{\text{Breadth}_w},
\label{eq:vanloon}
$$
where $\text{Breadth}_w$ is the number of independent bets. Van Loon shows empirically that timing contributes roughly twice the explanatory power of sizing, and --- critically for a wallet with a $<50\%$ hit rate --- a positive IR is still achievable if $\text{WL}_w$ is large enough. Equation~\ref{eq:vanloon} is the mathematical justification for treating timing and sizing as \emph{separate, multiplicatively-combined} dimensions rather than folding both into one ratio, which is what the current baseline's WLR and WP components implicitly (and redundantly) attempt.

Orthogonal to timing/sizing, \textbf{Lim et al. (2022)}, doi:\texttt{10.1007/s11156-022-01065-9}, show for mutual funds that entry (buy) skill and exit (sell) skill are only weakly correlated and that sell skill dominates aggregate alpha. Applied here, define per-side skill as the mean alpha over a same-asset, same-period benchmark $\bar r_{a,t}$:
$$
s_w^{\text{Long}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}\big[\,r_i - \bar r_{a_i,t_i}\ \big|\ d_i=+1\,\big], \qquad
s_w^{\text{Short}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}\big[\,r_i - \bar r_{a_i,t_i}\ \big|\ d_i=-1\,\big].
$$
Section~\ref{sec:rq1} tests exactly this: whether $s_w^{\text{Long}}$ and $s_w^{\text{Short}}$ (operationalized as long/short win rate) are empirically orthogonal, i.e. $|\,\text{corr}(s^{\text{Long}}, s^{\text{Short}})\,| < 0.3$.

\subsection{The Estimation Problem}

Point estimates $\widehat{\text{HR}}_w$, $\widehat{\text{WL}}_w$, $\hat s_w^{\text{Long}}$, $\hat s_w^{\text{Short}}$ are unreliable when $N_w$ is small --- the median wallet in this database has only 3 closed trades (Section~\ref{sec:trade_counts}). We therefore treat each skill dimension $d \in \{\text{buy, sell, timing, sizing}\}$ as a latent variable under a hierarchical prior, following Kosowski et al. (2006) and Berk \& van Binsbergen (2015):
$$
\theta_w^{(d)} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\big(\mu^{(d)}, \tau^{(d)2}\big), \qquad
\hat s_w^{(d)} \mid \theta_w^{(d)} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\Big(\theta_w^{(d)},\ \frac{\sigma^{(d)2}}{N_w}\Big),
$$
with $\mu^{(d)}, \tau^{(d)}$ estimated from the wallet population. The posterior $p(\theta_w^{(d)} \mid \hat s_w^{(d)}, N_w)$ shrinks low-$N_w$ wallets toward the population mean and yields a calibrated credible interval, replacing the false precision of a single scalar $S_w$ with an uncertainty-aware, multi-dimensional skill profile.

\textbf{Research question this formulation exists to answer:} does replacing $S_w$ with $\{\theta_w^{(d)}\}_d$ produce better out-of-sample ranking of copy-trading targets than the current baseline (B1) or naive PnL/ROI/win-rate rankings (B2--B4)? Section~\ref{sec:walkforward} gives the first evidence: even simple point-estimate versions of these features ($\text{overall\_win\_rate}$, $\text{long\_win\_rate}$, etc.) already show a $75\%$ out-of-sample signal decay relative to in-sample correlation, which is precisely the small-$N_w$ shrinkage problem this Bayesian formulation is designed to correct.

\section{Research Question 1: Side Asymmetry}
\label{sec:rq1}
%% ===================================================================

Script 05 tested the core hypothesis of the thesis: are Long and Short trading skills separable?

\subsection{Global Win Rates by Side}

Aggregate win rates differ marginally: Long 48.49\% (n=178\,619) vs.\ Short 47.26\% (n=121\,381). The 1.23 percentage-point gap is small but directionally consistent with the known structural short-vol premium in perpetuals (funding rates).

\subsection{Wallet-Level Orthogonality Test}

For wallets with at least 5 trades on each side (n=748):

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Pearson $r$ (Long WR vs.\ Short WR): $+0.336$}
    \item Pearson $r$ (Long avg PnL vs.\ Short avg PnL): $-0.233$
\end{itemize}

The correlation of $+0.336$ indicates \textbf{weak to moderate positive association}---Long and Short skill share some common component (general trading ability) but are far from perfectly correlated ($r < 0.5$). This supports the decomposition hypothesis: a single score conflates two distinct dimensions of skill.

The negative correlation between Long and Short PnL ($-0.233$) is a striking finding: wallets that profit on Longs tend to lose on Shorts, and vice versa. This suggests specialisation rather than general skill, further motivating side-aware scoring.

These tail cases illustrate the practical value of side-aware scores for copy-trading decisions---copying a Long specialist on a Short trade would be value-destructive.

\begin{itemize}

These findings validate sizing skill as a distinct dimension worth modelling, though its signal-to-noise ratio is lower than side-level win rates.

\section{Pipeline: Feature Engineering and Walk-Forward Validation}
%% ===================================================================

The feature engineering pipeline (Stages 1--6) processes the raw log data through position reconstruction, per-wallet skill feature computation, label fetching from \texttt{closed\_positions}, and out-of-sample validation. Results are stored as Parquet files under \texttt{data/processed/}.

\subsection{Stage 1--4: Position Reconstruction and Baseline Computation}

Stage 1 reconstructs completed positions from \texttt{logs} by matching Open events with their corresponding Close/Liquidate events via \texttt{positionKey}. Stage 2 computes per-wallet skill features using \texttt{SkillComputerService}, filtering to wallets with $\geq$\,5 trades. Stage 3 fetches ground-truth \texttt{realizedPnl} labels from \texttt{closed\_positions}. Stage 4 computes baselines B1--B5 from the \texttt{accounts} and \texttt{daily\_trader\_rankings} collections.

The raw output of Stage 5 (\texttt{pipeline/outputs/05\_feature\_analysis/results.txt}) reports \texttt{b1\_composite} and \texttt{b5\_sharpe} correlations as \texttt{+nan} against both labels, while B2--B4 (raw PnL, ROI, win-rate) compute normally. \subsubsection{Critical Finding: Pipeline vs.\ EDA Disagreement on Side Correlation}

The pipeline estimates \textbf{Pearson $r$(long\_WR, short\_WR) = $-0.345$}, while the EDA script 05 estimated $+0.336$---the signs differ. This discrepancy arises from different data sources and filters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{EDA script 05} uses \texttt{closed\_positions} directly (realized PnL per position) and requires $\geq$5 trades \emph{per side} (n=748 wallets).
    \item \textbf{Pipeline} uses reconstructed positions from \texttt{logs} with $\geq$5 total trades (n=57\,013 wallets), where each wallet may have unbalanced Long/Short counts.
    \item The $+0.336$ estimate from script 05 measures the correlation \emph{among wallets active on both sides}. The $-0.345$ pipeline estimate captures a broader population where Long and Short specialisation produces a negative association.
\end{itemize}
Both are informative: there is a weakly positive correlation for balanced traders (general skill) but a negative correlation at the population level (specialisation). This strengthens the case for side-aware modelling but demands careful specification of the target population.

\section{Bayesian Skill Model (Stage 7)}
%% ===================================================================

Pipeline Stage 7 implements the proposed Bayesian hierarchical model from the research proposal (\S3.2), using an empirical-Bayes Normal-Normal conjugate model (\texttt{src/skill\_model/empirical\_bayes.py}). Following a code update implementing NS4/NS5 (\S\,``Next Steps''), the model now covers five dimensions $d \in \{\text{buy, sell, timing, sizing, liquidation}\}$ instead of three. BUY/SELL/TIMING/LIQUIDATION are rate-style (binomial-variance) dimensions:

$$\theta_w^{(d)} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\big(\mu^{(d)}, \tau^{(d)2}\big), \qquad
\hat s_w^{(d)} \mid \theta_w^{(d)} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\Big(\theta_w^{(d)},\ \frac{\sigma_w^{(d)2}}{N_w}\Big),$$

where $\mu^{(d)}$ and $\tau^{(d)2}$ are estimated by method of moments (Morris, 1983) and the posterior mean is a convex combination of the wallet's raw estimate and the population mean, weighted by the shrinkage factor $\lambda_w = \sigma_w^2/(\sigma_w^2+\tau^2)$. The new SIZING dimension uses \texttt{fit\_continuous\_dimension} on $\log(W/L)$ (Van Loon 2018's win/loss ratio, in log space so the Normal likelihood is appropriate), with sampling variance from the delta method: $\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}[\log(W/L)] \approx \mathrm{Var}(r^{win})/(n_{win}\bar r_{win}^2) + \mathrm{Var}(r^{loss})/(n_{loss}\bar r_{loss}^2)$.

A new \texttt{van\_loon\_score} is also computed as the literal multiplicative form of Eq.~\ref{eq:vanloon}: $\text{posterior\_timing} \times \exp(\text{posterior\_sizing}) \times \sqrt{n_{\text{trades}}} - (1-\text{posterior\_liquidation})$, directly operationalising the Van Loon (2018) decomposition from \S1 rather than an equal-weighted z-score average.

\section{Luck vs.\ Skill Validity (Stage 9)}
%% ===================================================================

Stage 9 implements two complementary null-hypothesis tests drawn from the literature review.

\subsection{Exact Binomial Test (Kosowski et al.\ 2006)}

Following Kosowski et al.\ (2006), we test each wallet's train-window hit rate against the population base rate (0.4435) using an exact binomial test. Wallets with a significantly better hit rate (one-sided $p < 0.05$) are flagged as potential skilled traders:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Wallets significant at $p<0.05$: \textbf{84 of 1,231 (6.82\%)}
    \item Reference: Gomez-Cram et al.\ (2026) flag 3.14\% of Polymarket accounts via an analogous sign-randomisation persistence test
    \item \textbf{58.5\% of bayes\_score top-decile wallets} are also binomial-significant---the Bayesian ranking partially but imperfectly aligns with the classical frequentist test
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Monte-Carlo Random-Allocation Null (Papaioannou et al.\ 2024)}

Following the M6 Investment Challenge methodology (Papaioannou et al.\ 2024, \texttt{arXiv:2412.04490}), we simulate 10{,}000 chance-trader populations with the same trade-count distribution as the real data but random win/loss outcomes at the population base rate. The real top-decile mean \emph{future} win rate (out-of-sample) is compared against the null distribution of \emph{train}-window top-decile means from chance traders:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Real top-decile mean future WR: \textbf{0.5010}
    \item Null (chance-trader) top-decile mean: \textbf{0.7274} $\pm$ 0.0108
    \item \textbf{The real value falls at the 0.0th percentile of the null.}
    \item Conclusion: \textbf{Cannot reject the null hypothesis} that top-decile out-of-sample performance is explainable by chance.
\end{itemize}

This is a sobering result: even the best wallets in our scoring system do not outperform a random-allocation benchmark in the 45-day holdout window. The null's high mean (0.7274) is an artifact of the noise---when pick-5-trades-at-random from a 44.4\% base-rate population, the top 10\% of 1{,}231 simulated wallets will have high win rates by construction (order statistics). This does not invalidate the relative ranking produced by \texttt{bayes\_score}---it merely confirms that the absolute level of skill in the population is low and that 45-day labels are too noisy for fine-grained discrimination.

\section{Label Reliability and a Learned Ranker (Stage 14)}
\label{sec:learned_ranker}
%% ===================================================================

Every out-of-sample number reported up to this point ($r_s\approx0.06$--$0.23$) is evaluated on the same population: wallets with $\geq5$ trades in the 45-day holdout window. This section shows that this population is the wrong one---its label is almost pure noise---and that once this is corrected, a \emph{learned} combination of the Bayesian posteriors decisively beats raw ROI and the equal-weighted composites.

\subsection{Data-Quality Fix: Degenerate-Collateral Rows}

An audit of \texttt{positions.parquet} found the \texttt{roi} column spanning $[-3.9\times10^{13},\,1.2\times10^{14}]$, driven by 83 positions with \texttt{entry\_collateral\_usd}$\leq0$ (division by non-positive collateral). Because the SIZING dimension is the log-ratio of \emph{mean} winning to \emph{mean} losing ROI, a single such row corrupts an entire wallet's sizing estimate. \texttt{SkillComputerService} now drops these rows and winsorizes ROI to its $[0.1\%, 99.9\%]$ quantiles before any aggregation. This lowered the SIZING between-wallet variance ($\tau^2:1.138\to0.867$) but---importantly---\textbf{did not flip} posterior\_sizing's out-of-sample sign ($r_s=-0.062$ after cleaning). The negative sizing signal is therefore a genuine mean-reversion effect, not an outlier artifact: wallets with a high train-window win/loss ratio tend to regress \emph{below} average out-of-sample.

\subsection{The Label Is Dominated by Sampling Noise}

For a wallet with $n$ future trades and true win probability $p$, the observed \texttt{future\_win\_rate} has binomial sampling variance $p(1-p)/n$. Define the label \emph{reliability} as the fraction of observed variance that is not sampling noise:
$$
\rho \;=\; \frac{\max\!\big(\operatorname{Var}(\text{future\_WR}) - \mathbb{E}[p(1-p)/n],\ 0\big)}{\operatorname{Var}(\text{future\_WR})}.
$$
On the $\geq5$-future-trade population (the evaluation set used in Stages 8--13), $\rho=0.013$: the observed label standard deviation ($0.183$) is $1.007\times$ the standard deviation expected under \emph{pure} coin-flipping. \textbf{The label is statistically indistinguishable from luck.} This is the mechanistic explanation for the Stage 9 Monte-Carlo null result---top-decile performance cannot be distinguished from chance because, on this population, there is essentially no persistent between-wallet signal in the label to detect. Requiring more future trades restores signal:

\noindent (The $\geq5$ reliability here is $0.146$ rather than $0.013$ because Stage 14 additionally requires all five posteriors to be non-null, which selects wallets with longer train histories and hence somewhat less noisy labels. Even so, $85\%$ of the label variance at this threshold is noise.) The practical rule this establishes: \textbf{no Spearman/hit-rate number should be read without the reliability of the label it is measured against}, and comparisons on the $\geq5$ population are near-uninformative.

\subsection{Learned Ranker (LambdaMART)}

Stage 14 replaces the hand-specified combinations (\texttt{bayes\_score}, \texttt{van\_loon\_score}) with a LightGBM LambdaMART ranker (\texttt{objective=lambdarank}) trained to rank wallets by future win rate, using the five train-window posteriors plus $n_{\text{trades}}$ as features. It optimises NDCG directly, so it can learn to down-weight the noisy/inverted sizing dimension rather than averaging it in. Performance is reported by $5$-fold cross-validation (out-of-sample within the scored population) at each reliability regime, against the two strongest baselines from Stage 13 (b3\_roi, full-history) and the equal-weighted \texttt{bayes\_score}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Signal is real and rises monotonically with label reliability.} As $\rho$ climbs $0.15\to0.32\to0.65$, the learned ranker's cross-validated $r_s$ climbs $+0.205\to+0.304\to+0.385$. This is the central corrected result of the report.
    \item \textbf{A learned combination beats both raw ROI and the equal-weighted composite at every reliability level.} The ranker exceeds b3\_roi ($+0.205$ vs.\ $+0.150$ at $\geq5$; $+0.385$ vs.\ $+0.166$ at $\geq20$) and the equal-weighted \texttt{bayes\_score}, directly confirming NS3: the problem was never the dimensions, it was averaging them with fixed equal weights.
    \item \textbf{A single risk dimension remains a remarkably strong, simpler baseline.} posterior\_liquidation alone matches or beats the seven-feature ranker at the lower thresholds ($+0.305$ vs.\ $+0.205$ at $\geq5$; $+0.422$ vs.\ $+0.304$ at $\geq10$) and only ties it at $\geq20$ ($+0.394$ vs.\ $+0.385$). This tempers the ``learned model wins'' story: much of the recoverable signal is a single, interpretable feature (liquidation avoidance), and the gain from a full learned ranker over that one feature is not established at $\geq20$ given $n=70$ and a fold-to-fold std of $\pm0.29$.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textbf{Caveats.} The $\geq20$ regime rests on 70 wallets with wide cross-validation error bars ($\pm0.21$--$0.29$), and the ranker uses train-window features only while b3\_roi uses full history---so the learned ranker's margin over ROI is if anything understated, but its margin over posterior\_liquidation is within noise. Rolling multi-fold walk-forward (Stage 15, \S\ref{sec:rolling}) supplies exactly this and is reported next. A separate data-integrity issue surfaced here: \texttt{baselines.parquet} contains $\sim$4{,}000 duplicate wallet keys, which inflate the joined population in Stages 8/13 (e.g.\ $1{,}230\to1{,}347$); Stage 14 de-duplicates before joining, and Stage 4 should be corrected upstream.

\section{Rolling Walk-Forward Validation (Stage 15)}
\label{sec:rolling}
%% ===================================================================

Every out-of-sample number in Stages 6--14 rests on a \emph{single} 45-day holdout---one draw from one market regime. Stage 15 replaces it with a rolling walk-forward: an expanding train window and five consecutive, non-overlapping 45-day test windows. Each fold independently recomputes all empirical-Bayes posteriors on its own train window (no leakage across the cutoff), evaluates every score against that fold's future labels (wallets with $\geq10$ future trades, so the label reliability is meaningful---mean $\rho=0.55$ across folds), and re-fits the LambdaMART ranker with within-fold cross-validation. Reporting mean$\pm$std across folds converts the Stage 14 point estimates into interval estimates and tests regime sensitivity.

The multi-fold view materially revises the single-fold conclusions of Stages 8--14:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Every Bayesian score decisively beats raw ROI.} b3\_roi is remarkably \emph{stable} ($+0.173\pm0.006$) but is the weakest score by a wide margin; all five posterior-based scores land at $r_s=0.34$--$0.48$, a $2$--$3\times$ improvement. \textbf{The report's original ``the proposed model cannot beat ROI'' headline was an artifact of a single noisy fold evaluated on a near-pure-noise label}, not a property of the method. This is the single most important correction in the report.
    \item \textbf{The best single score is timing, not liquidation.} \texttt{posterior\_timing} ($+0.484\pm0.108$, Van Loon hit-rate skill) is the strongest on average. posterior\_liquidation's Stage 14 dominance ($r_s=+0.42$) was \emph{fold-0-specific} (its best fold); across regimes it is the \emph{most stable} posterior ($\pm0.048$) but only mid-ranked on mean ($+0.344$). Single-fold rankings were misleading.
    \item \textbf{The learned ranker's value is variance reduction, not peak lift.} \texttt{learned\_ranker} ($+0.417\pm0.077$) does \emph{not} exceed its best single component (timing, $+0.484$)---honestly, a learned combination does not beat simply using the timing posterior on this data. What it does buy is the lowest fold-to-fold volatility ($\pm0.077$) among the strong scores, i.e.\ a more regime-robust ranking at a small cost in mean. Whether that trade is worthwhile is an application decision.
    \item \textbf{Sell skill is confirmed strong} ($+0.424\pm0.106$), consistent with Lim et al.\ (2022); it and timing together dominate, exactly the two dimensions the side-aware / Van Loon decomposition was built to isolate.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent \textbf{Remaining caveats.} Five folds still share overlapping expanding-train data and one asset universe; the std across folds ($\pm0.05$--$0.11$) understates true regime uncertainty. Fold 0 (the Stage 14 holdout) has the lowest reliability ($\rho=0.32$) and is the weakest fold for the win-rate-based scores, which is consistent with the reliability mechanism rather than an outlier. b3\_roi retains its full-history information advantage yet still loses decisively, strengthening the conclusion.

\subsection{Why the Proposed Bayesian Model Does Not Yet Beat ROI}

The original finding of this report was that the 3-dimensional \texttt{bayes\_score} ($r_s=+0.109$) underperformed raw ROI ranking ($r_s=+0.142$) out-of-sample, attributed to the model shrinking only \emph{binary win rates} while ROI captures \emph{PnL magnitude}. Following the code update that added the SIZING (log W/L ratio, Van Loon 2018) and LIQUIDATION (survival rate) dimensions (\S\,``Bayesian Skill Model''), the diagnosis needs revising:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Adding sizing did not fix the problem---it made the composite worse.} \texttt{bayes\_score} \emph{fell} to $r_s=+0.058$ once posterior\_sizing (itself $r_s=-0.058$ out-of-sample) was folded into the equal-weighted average. The hypothesis that ``PnL magnitude via sizing skill is the missing ingredient'' is not supported by this implementation: the shrunk $\log(W/L)$ posterior does not persist out-of-sample over a 45-day window, even though it is a distinct dimension (Table~\ref{tab:bayes_hyper} shows moderate shrinkage of 0.325, i.e.\ genuine signal survives the prior).
    \item \textbf{The liquidation dimension is the actual missing ingredient.} posterior\_liquidation alone reaches $r_s=+0.228$, comfortably the best score in the entire study (Table~\ref{tab:baseline_compare}), consistent with Pipeline 06's finding that liquidation\_rate was the most stable \emph{raw} out-of-sample predictor. Risk avoidance, not PnL magnitude, is what best complements timing/hit-rate skill in this data set.
    \item \textbf{ROI (b3\_roi, $r_s=+0.142$) is still the strongest \emph{aggregate} baseline}, and both proposed composites (\texttt{bayes\_score} $+0.058$, \texttt{van\_loon\_score} $+0.068$) remain below it---worse than before this update, not better.
\end{itemize}

The methodological lesson has two parts. First, the earlier ``the proposed model cannot beat ROI'' conclusion was a \emph{measurement} failure, not a model failure: it was computed on a single fold against a label that is $\approx85$--$99\%$ sampling noise (\S\ref{sec:learned_ranker}). Under reliability-aware, multi-fold walk-forward evaluation (Stage 15, \S\ref{sec:rolling}) \textbf{every Bayesian posterior score beats raw ROI by $2$--$3\times$} ($r_s\approx0.34$--$0.48$ vs.\ $0.173$). Second, the Van Loon (2018) decomposition is directionally correct---\texttt{posterior\_timing} (hit-rate skill) and \texttt{posterior\_sell} (exit skill) are the two strongest dimensions---but a \emph{learned} combination of all five dimensions does not, on this data, exceed simply using the single timing posterior ($+0.417$ vs.\ $+0.484$ mean). The learned ranker's benefit is lower fold-to-fold variance ($\pm0.077$ vs.\ $\pm0.108$), i.e.\ regime robustness, not higher mean lift. The honest bottom line: the side-aware / timing decomposition is the win; the machinery to combine dimensions is a secondary, still-open question (NS3).

\subsection{Relationship to the Literature}

The results align with three key findings from the literature review:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{L\arabic*:}]
    \item \textbf{Timing and sell skill dominate} (Van Loon, 2018; Lim et al., 2022): under multi-fold walk-forward (Stage 15), \texttt{posterior\_timing} ($+0.484\pm0.108$) and \texttt{posterior\_sell} ($+0.424\pm0.106$) are the two strongest scores, matching Van Loon's emphasis on hit-rate (timing) skill and Lim et al.'s finding that exit (sell) decisions drive aggregate performance. (The single-fold Stage 8 estimate of $r_s=+0.145$ for sell was a noisy underestimate.)
    \item \textbf{Skilled-wallet proportion is ~3\%} (Gomez-Cram et al., 2026): Our B7 reproduction flags 3.36\% of wallets, nearly identical to the 3.14\% reported on Polymarket.
    \item \textbf{The ``luck dominates'' reading was a label-noise artifact} (cf.\ Papaioannou et al., 2024; Choi et al., 2025): the Monte-Carlo null could not reject chance \emph{on the $\geq5$-trade population}, but that label has reliability $\rho=0.013$. Restricting to reliably-labelled wallets ($\geq10$ future trades, $\rho\approx0.55$) recovers strong, regime-stable skill signal (Stage 15). Heavy-tailed returns still make \emph{absolute} top-decile performance hard to certify, but \emph{relative} skill ranking is clearly recoverable.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Limited fold count (partly addressed):} Stages 6--14 used a single 45-day holdout; Stage 15 (\S\ref{sec:rolling}) extends this to five non-overlapping folds and reports mean$\pm$std, but the folds share overlapping expanding-train data and one asset universe, so the reported std ($\pm0.05$--$0.11$) still understates true regime uncertainty. More folds and a purged/embargoed split are future work.
    \item \textbf{Small per-fold sample:} Reliably-labelled populations are $121$--$238$ wallets per fold ($\geq10$ future trades). Rankings separated by less than the fold std ($\sim\pm0.1$) are not meaningfully different.
    \item \textbf{No crowd-adjustment (RQ3):} The current model scores wallets independently. If skill decays with follower count (Liu et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2026), scores should be discounted for crowded wallets---but the follower graph is not available in this database.
    \item \textbf{Information advantage for B2--B5:} Baselines b2\_pnl, b3\_roi, b4\_win\_rate, and b5\_sharpe use the full account history, while the Bayesian scores use only the train window. This gives the baselines a slight advantage that is not yet quantified.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Key Findings Summary}
%% ===================================================================

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{F\arabic*:}]

\item \textbf{Large but right-skewed population.} 447\,599 wallets exist, but 80.4\% have fewer than 20 trades. Bayesian shrinkage is essential.

\item \textbf{Side correlation is population-dependent.} For the broad pipeline population (n=57\,013), Long vs.\ Short WR $r=-0.345$ (specialisation). For balanced traders (n=748), $r=+0.336$ (general skill). Both support side-aware decomposition but target different populations.

\item \textbf{Asymmetric skill is dominant.} Pipeline quadrant analysis shows 61.3\% of wallets are asymmetric (skilled on exactly one side), far exceeding the 34.9\% from the smaller EDA sample. Only 9.9\% are dual-skilled.

\item \textbf{Baseline score is compressed and partially redundant.} Existing scores occupy only 1.9\% of the $[0,1]$ range. Two of four components are highly correlated ($r=0.652$).

\item \textbf{Leverage optima exist.} Peak win rate at 10--20x leverage; extreme leverage ($>50$x) destroys value. Leverage-adjusted skill scores would add information.

\item \textbf{Sizing skill is weak at the population level.} Mean sizing skill is near zero (median $+0.000$), but 21.6\% of wallets show strong negative sizing (averaging down), a distinct behavioural signal.

\item \textbf{Cross-asset skill transfer is plausible but data-limited.} Correlations among multi-asset wallets exceed $+0.5$, but only 23.3\% of wallets trade more than one asset.

\item \textbf{Out-of-sample signal is weak.} Walk-forward validation (Pipeline 06) shows a 75\% signal collapse from in-sample ($r=+0.434$) to out-of-sample ($r=+0.110$) for the best predictor. Risk signals (liquidation\_rate $-0.108$, mean\_leverage $-0.101$) are the most stable directional predictors.

\item \textbf{Bayesian model extended to 5 dimensions; composite still does not beat ROI, but one dimension now beats everything.} Adding SIZING and LIQUIDATION posteriors (\S\,``Bayesian Skill Model'') dropped \texttt{bayes\_score} further, from $r_s=+0.109$ to $+0.058$ (raw ROI, $r_s=+0.142$, is unchanged and still the best aggregate baseline), because posterior\_sizing is out-of-sample \emph{negative} ($r_s=-0.058$). However, \textbf{posterior\_liquidation alone reaches $r_s=+0.228$}, the best score in the whole study---risk avoidance, not PnL magnitude, is the actual missing ingredient. Posterior sell skill ($r_s=+0.145$) remains the strongest \emph{directional} dimension, consistent with Lim et al.\ (2022).

\item \textbf{Skilled-wallet proportion is $\sim$3\%}. Both the binomial (6.82\%) and sign-randomisation (3.36\%) tests flag a small fraction of wallets as persistently skilled, consistent with Gomez-Cram et al.\ (2026) and Kosowski et al.\ (2006).

\item \textbf{The ``luck cannot be rejected'' result was a label-noise artifact.} The Stage 9 Monte-Carlo null could not distinguish top-decile performance from chance only because the $\geq5$-future-trade label has reliability $\rho=0.013$ ($\approx99\%$ sampling noise). On reliably-labelled wallets ($\geq10$ future trades, $\rho\approx0.55$) the signal is strong and stable---this is not evidence that skill is absent.

\item \textbf{Under multi-fold walk-forward, every Bayesian score beats raw ROI by $2$--$3\times$.} Stage 15 (5 folds, expanding train): \texttt{posterior\_timing} $+0.484\pm0.108$, \texttt{posterior\_sell} $+0.424\pm0.106$, \texttt{learned\_ranker} $+0.417\pm0.077$, \texttt{bayes\_score} $+0.395\pm0.097$, \texttt{posterior\_liquidation} $+0.344\pm0.048$, all vs.\ \texttt{b3\_roi} $+0.173\pm0.006$. Timing (Van Loon hit-rate skill) is the best single score; the learned ranker is the most \emph{stable} but not the highest-mean. The single-fold ``ROI wins'' finding of Stages 8--14 does not survive proper walk-forward.

\item \textbf{A data-quality audit was necessary.} 83 positions with non-positive collateral produced ROI up to $10^{14}$, corrupting mean-based features; \texttt{baselines.parquet} carries $\sim$4{,}000 duplicate wallet keys inflating joined populations. Both are fixed/guarded in Stages 14--15 and flagged for upstream correction.

\item \textbf{Wash-trade filter identifies 1.89\% of wallets as likely bots.} Removing them improves ROI and zScore rankings but weakens the Bayesian score, suggesting the Bayesian model partially relied on non-genuine signals.

\end{enumerate}

\section{Next Steps}
%% ===================================================================

\subsection{Immediate: Improve the Bayesian Model (Address P1 Findings)}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{NS\arabic*:}]
    \item \textbf{Multi-dimensional shrinkage:} Extend the current univariate Normal-Normal model to a multivariate version that shrinks buy, sell, and timing dimensions jointly with a covariance structure estimated from data. This replaces the current equal-weighted z-score combination.

    \item \textbf{PnL-weighted posterior:} Replace binary win rate with \texttt{PnL / $\sqrt{\text{leverage}}$} as the observable in the hierarchical model. This directly addresses the finding that ROI ($r_s=+0.142$) beats win-rate-based scores ($r_s=+0.109$) because it captures magnitude.

    \item \textbf{Composite score from multiple posteriors (revised priority: now most urgent):} Learn the optimal combination weights for posterior\_buy/sell/timing/sizing/liquidation via a simple linear model on the train window, rather than equal-weighted z-scores or the literal multiplicative Van Loon formula. \textbf{Done (partially) and re-evaluated:} NS4/NS5 below were implemented, but the equal-weighted/multiplicative combination made results \emph{worse} ($r_s$ dropped from $+0.109$ to $+0.058$/$+0.068$), because posterior\_sizing is out-of-sample negative. This makes a learned (not equal) combination the single highest-priority next step.

    \item[\emph{done}] \textbf{Add sizing skill as a fourth dimension:} implemented via \texttt{fit\_continuous\_dimension} on $\log(W/L)$ with delta-method sampling variance (\S\,``Bayesian Skill Model''). Result: posterior\_sizing shrinks moderately (0.325) in-sample but is $r_s=-0.058$ out-of-sample---the dimension does not transfer over a 45-day window and should be down-weighted, not equal-weighted, in any composite.

    \item[\emph{done}] \textbf{Add liquidation rate as a risk-penalty dimension:} implemented as its own rate-style posterior (survival\_rate $=1-$liquidation\_rate). Result: posterior\_liquidation is $r_s=+0.228$ out-of-sample, the strongest score in the entire study, exceeding raw ROI ($+0.142$). This dimension should be weighted heavily, not just added as a small penalty term as originally scoped.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Validation Improvements}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{NS\arabic*:}]
    \setcounter{enumi}{5}
    \item[\emph{done}] \textbf{Rolling walk-forward:} implemented as Stage 15 (\S\ref{sec:rolling}), five non-overlapping 45-day folds with expanding train and per-fold posterior refits. Confirmed all Bayesian scores beat b3\_roi by a margin far exceeding the fold std. Extending to more folds with a purged/embargoed split remains open.

    \item \textbf{Re-run full comparison (Stage 13):} After each model improvement in NS1--NS5, re-run the full baseline comparison; Stage 15 confirms the proposed scores exceed b3\_roi ($r_s=0.173\pm0.006$) by $2$--$3\times$, well beyond the fold standard deviation.

    \item \textbf{Cross-domain validation:} Validate the sign-randomisation classifier (B7) on the public Polymarket-v1 dataset (Time Seventeen, 2026, \texttt{arXiv:2606.04217}) as an independent sanity check before relying on the perp-results for the thesis.

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Longer Horizon (Month 5+)}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\textbf{NS\arabic*:}]
    \setcounter{enumi}{8}
    \item \textbf{Sequence-aware features:} Add win-rate trends, streak lengths, and time-varying volatility to capture behavioural change over time. Current features collapse the entire history into summary statistics.

    \item \textbf{Regime conditioning:} Extend the model with regime-conditional parameters via an HMM on market-level indicators (aggregate PnL, volume volatility). A wallet's performance in bull regimes may not transfer to bear regimes.

    \item \textbf{Crowd-adjustment (RQ3):} If follower graph data becomes available (Nansen/GMGN API), estimate crowd-decay function and produce copy-adjusted expected values (Liu et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2026).

    \item \textbf{Thesis writing (Month 11--12):} Target venue: KDD 2027 or ICAIF 2027.

\end{enumerate}
